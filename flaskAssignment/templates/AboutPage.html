About our site

This site is designed for those who are, for one reason or another, intrigued by Kickstarter, to learn about what factors—goals, time period, type of project, etc—result in failed or successful Kickstarter projects. Our goal is to help you learn about trends among Kickstarter projects, and if you have a project vision, to help you see how similar projects have performed historically. Whether you are a curious user, a crowdfunding enthusiast, or an entrepreneur, we welcome you; this site has something for everyone.

The dataset we use contains over 300,000 Kickstarter projects dating up until 2018. The data is sorted into individual projects with a variety of attributes. These include things like the project name, the category (i.e. Film & Video, Music, Food), the start date and deadlines, the number of backers, and other important statistics. We have slighty modified the original dataset to make it easier to implement on the website.

About our Analysis

There are a couple of elements on our site that use either analysis conducted on R software or custom parameters we have created.

Our Create and Contrast tab uses a binomial generalized linear model to conduct our linear regression and calculate a probability a project will be successful given certain variables. The three independent and significant variables we found were usd_goal, currency, and category. Some parameters that were significant were omitted due to their relation with other variables. For example, currency and country were both significant but directly related to each other, and thus only one could be used. We chose to include currency over country as it is more prominent on a project’s proposal. It is important to note that this model is not conclusive and by no means exact in predicting the probability of success. It is an estimate based on parameters given but does not take into account the quality of a project or the publicity that it might receive, for example.

Under our Explore page, users can create their own lists of successful projects given certain parameters. To determine how successful a project is, we formulated success scores by transforming the number of backers with the natural logarithm and adding this to the proportion of USD pledged funds to USD goal funds. To account for projects, we also had to add one backer to every project so that the logarithm would be defined everywhere.
